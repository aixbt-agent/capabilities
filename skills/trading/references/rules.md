# Trading Rules: Complete Reference

Comprehensive ruleset organized by topic. The parent `SKILL.md` is the actionable summary; this file is the deep-dive library.

## Risk Management

**The 1% rule:**
- Risk no more than 1% of portfolio on any single trade
- Risk an equal amount on every trade. Never let one bet jeopardize the whole account
- "I try to keep my risk per trade at a maximum of around 1% of my equity"

**Position sizing formula:**
- `risk_amount / (entry - stop) = position size`
- Whatever position size feels right, cut it in half. "Undertrade, undertrade, undertrade"
- Define risk before entering a trade. Know exactly where you're getting out before you get in

**Drawdown tiers:**
- 5% drawdown: reduce position sizes by 25-50%
- 8% drawdown: reduce further, limit new positions
- 15% drawdown: stop trading entirely. Reassess everything
- "The first goal after a drawdown is not to make it back. It's to stop losing"
- Small wins rebuild confidence. Don't try to get it all back in one trade

**Daily and monthly limits:**
- 4% daily loss limit: stop trading for the day if hit
- 10% monthly loss limit: stop and reassess if hit

**Equity curve management:**
- If equity curve falls below its own 200-day moving average, halt trading
- Monitor the system itself, not just the trades

**Capital preservation:**
- If you lose all your chips, you can't bet. Capital preservation comes first
- You should always have a worst case point. The only choice should be to get out quicker
- The most important rule of trading is to play great defense, not great offense

## Trend Following

**Trend alignment:**
- Trade with the trend. Never fight the dominant direction
- Long-term trend is the most important factor, then current chart pattern, then picking a good entry point
- Never trade counter-trend on the dominant timeframe

**Breakout systems:**
- 20-day breakout: enter when price exceeds the high/low of the last 20 days
- 55-day breakout: the primary signal for major trend changes
- Ride the trend until it bends. Don't predict, react

**The 200-day moving average:**
- The 200-day moving average is the single best indicator of a major trend
- When the 200-day moving average is declining, pay attention
- Everything gets destroyed a hundred times faster than it is built up

**Counter-trend warning:**
- "What feels good is often the wrong thing to do"
- Human nature does not operate to maximize gain but rather to maximize the chance of gain
- People want to be right more than they want to make money. This is why most traders lose

**SEPA criteria (Specific Entry Point Analysis):**
- 50-day SMA > 150-day SMA > 200-day SMA (all rising)
- Price above 50-day and 200-day SMA
- 200-day SMA trending up for at least 1 month (preferably 4-5 months)
- Relative strength rating of 70+
- Current price within 25% of 52-week high
- Current price at least 30% above 52-week low

## Entry Discipline

**Three-confirmation rule:**
- Never trade unless fundamentals, technicals, and market sentiment all align
- "When in doubt, get out. Don't enter a trade until the light is green"
- Patience is the hardest part. Wait for the setup, not the excitement

**Invalidation:**
- Define invalidation before entry. At what specific level or event is the thesis wrong?
- Treat every trade as a hypothesis, not a conviction. Hypotheses get tested and discarded
- "If you go in with a plan and know what would prove you wrong, you take the emotions out of it"

**Hypothesis-driven trading:**
- Don't try to predict. Identify the current regime and trade accordingly
- The market doesn't care about your analysis. Only price pays

**Patience:**
- "Wait until there is money lying in the corner, and all you have to do is go over there and pick it up"
- Don't trade for the sake of trading. Most of the time, the best trade is no trade
- Do your own homework. Consensus opinions are already priced in

## Exit Discipline

**Stop mechanics:**
- Place your stop at a point that, if reached, will reasonably indicate the trade is wrong
- Stop loss: 7-8% below entry as a hard maximum. No exceptions
- Never move a stop further from entry
- Never average losers

**Time stops:**
- If a trade hasn't moved in your favor within a defined period, exit
- Set daily loss limits. If hit, stop trading for the day

**Weekend rule:**
- Close losing positions before weekends to avoid gap risk

**Tightening stops and partial profits:**
- Tighten stop when position reaches 30% of profit target
- Take partial profits at 1% of total equity
- Factor of 3 rule: the completion target of a chart pattern is one-third of the time it took to form

**Letting winners run:**
- Ride your winners. Don't cut them short
- Professionals go broke by taking small profits. Amateurs go broke by taking large losses
- The win/loss ratio is completely unimportant. It is the magnitude of the gain versus the magnitude of the loss that matters
- "Most of my best trades, my largest winners, worked right from the start"

**Journaling:**
- Keep a trading journal. Review it. Patterns emerge that you can't see in real-time
- Learn from losses. Write down what went wrong. Don't repeat the same mistake

## Position Sizing

**Undertrade principle:**
- Whatever position size feels right, cut it in half
- Trade small enough to survive, big enough to make a difference

**Volatility-based sizing:**
- Adjust size inversely to recent volatility
- Consistency comes from having a defined process and sticking to it

**Scale-in:**
- 50% at entry, 30% on confirmation, 20% on momentum
- Scale in and out. Don't go all-in at once

**Conviction-based sizing:**
- Position size should reflect conviction level
- "The really good traders are able to be aggressive when the trade is working and pull back when it's not"
- Sit on your hands at least 50% of the time. Most market conditions aren't worth trading
- "You can't consistently be a winning trader if you're banking on being right more than 50% of the time"

## Asymmetry and Convexity

**Option-like payoffs:**
- The ideal trade costs little to be wrong and pays enormously to be right
- Small, defined-risk positions in asymmetric setups compound into massive returns over time
- "The option buyer's maximum loss is known and limited to the premium paid"
- Don't sell optionality (unlimited downside for limited premium) unless you have specific structural reasons
- Many of the best trades look like they'll probably lose but have enormous payoff if they win

**Barbell strategy:**
- Combine extremely safe positions (90%) with small, high-optionality bets (10%). Avoid the middle
- Keep the majority of capital in the safest possible position (stables, hard assets)
- Allocate a small, expendable portion to high-upside asymmetric bets
- Never be "medium" safe. The middle is where silent risk accumulates
- "If you put 90% of funds in boring cash and 10% in very risky securities, you cannot possibly lose more than 10%, while you are exposed to massive upside"
- Each small bet should have the potential to return multiples on the total portfolio, not just on the bet itself

**Convex positions:**
- Prefer positions with convex payoffs: limited downside, unlimited upside
- Favor assets and protocols that get stronger through stress-testing and attacks
- Avoid positions where increasing volatility hurts you (concave payoffs)
- "Wind extinguishes a candle and energizes fire. You want to be the fire"

**Capped downside:**
- Target risk-reward of at least 5:1
- Minimum 3:1 risk-reward on every trade. No exceptions

## Fragility and Tail Risk

**Fat tails:**
- Rare, high-impact events dominate outcomes in complex systems. Standard models dramatically underestimate their frequency
- Historical volatility underestimates future risk because it excludes the events that haven't happened yet
- "The problem is not that tail events happen. The problem is that people don't believe they will"
- Never use Value-at-Risk or any model that assumes normally distributed returns for crypto
- Position for the possibility that tomorrow looks nothing like today
- Your model of the world is incomplete. Always account for what you don't know

**Turkey problem:**
- A long track record of stability can mask hidden risk rather than prove safety
- "Consider a turkey that is fed every day. Every single feeding will firm up the bird's belief that it is the general rule of life to be fed every day"
- The longer a yield source has paid steadily without incident, the more suspicious you should be, not less
- Ask: what is the mechanism? If you can't explain why it works, you may be the turkey
- Stablecoins depegging, protocols being exploited, and "safe" yields collapsing are all turkey events

**Fragility detection:**
- Test a thesis by asking: what happens if conditions get 2x worse? 5x worse? 10x worse?
- If a position depends on a specific prediction being correct, it is fragile
- If a position depends on volatility staying low, it is fragile
- If a position depends on liquidity being available, it is fragile
- Fragile positions tend to give small, steady returns punctuated by catastrophic losses

**Ruin avoidance:**
- Expected value means nothing if any single outcome can wipe you out
- "If you have a 1% chance of ruin per year, you will almost certainly be ruined over a long career"
- Never, under any circumstances, take a bet that has any probability of total loss, no matter how small
- Size positions so that the worst realistic outcome is survivable
- What happens to a group taking a risk once is different from what happens to one person taking the risk repeatedly. One instance of ruin ends the game permanently

**Extremistan:**
- Crypto is Extremistan. A single day can deliver more return (or loss) than the entire previous year
- Statistical tools designed for normal distributions (standard deviation, Sharpe ratio, correlation) break in extreme environments
- Don't use averages or "typical" returns as planning tools. The outliers dominate
- Plan for the outlier, not the average. The average is a statistical ghost that never actually occurs

## Cognitive Biases

**Narrative fallacy:**
- A compelling story about why an asset will rise is not evidence it will rise
- Post-hoc narratives about why the market moved are almost always fabricated
- Separate the structural payoff of a position from the story supporting it
- The more coherent and convincing a market narrative sounds, the more suspicious you should be

**Survivorship bias:**
- For every 100x token, there are thousands of identical-looking tokens that went to zero
- Don't extrapolate from success stories. Study the failures that had the same initial characteristics
- "The cemetery of failed projects is silent. You don't hear from the dead"
- Selection bias makes every strategy look better in backtests than in practice
- When someone tells you about a great trade, ask: how many similar trades failed?

**Signal vs noise:**
- Hourly and daily price movements are dominated by noise, not signal
- The more frequently you check prices, the more noise you consume and the worse your decisions become
- Increasing your information consumption does not improve your decision quality
- Focus on structural factors (trend, on-chain flows, protocol fundamentals) over price ticks and social sentiment

**Green lumber fallacy:**
- Technical understanding of a protocol is not the same as understanding its price dynamics
- The best traders often can't articulate why their edge works in academic terms
- Practical market knowledge (order flow, liquidity, positioning) may matter more than fundamental analysis
- Don't confuse narrative understanding with predictive power

**Skin in the game:**
- Don't trust analysis from people who don't bear the consequences of being wrong
- Discount advice from commentators, analysts, and influencers who face no consequences for bad calls
- "Never ask anyone for their opinion, forecast, or recommendation. Just ask them what they have in their portfolio"
- The best signal comes from people who are betting their own capital

## Psychology and Discipline

**Ego:**
- "Don't be a hero. Don't have an ego. Always question yourself and your ability"
- The confidence to trade comes from preparation, not from results
- If you personalize losses, you can't trade
- "The moment you think you're good is the moment you get hurt"

**Process over outcome:**
- A good trade can lose money; a bad trade can make money
- Process quality matters more than outcome quality
- There is no single market secret; what works is hard work, discipline, and risk management
- All great traders have a method; the specifics vary but the discipline is universal

**Emotional discipline:**
- Emotional discipline is the key to successful trading, not intelligence
- The biggest losses come from positions that violate your own rules
- When good traders have losing positions, they get out. When bad traders have losing positions, they pray
- Most traders lose because they'd rather lose money than admit they're wrong

**Sitting out:**
- Sitting out is a position. No obligation to always have a take
- Wait for high-conviction setups. Most of the time, do nothing
- Excitement is the wrong reason to trade

**Overtrading harm:**
- Every trade has transaction costs, slippage, and attention costs
- The urge to "do something" during drawdowns is the most expensive impulse in trading
- Doing nothing is almost always better than doing something unless you have a clear, high-conviction reason
- Most portfolio damage comes from overreaction, not from the original move

**Journaling:**
- Keep a trading journal. Review it. Patterns emerge that you can't see in real-time
- Learn from losses. Write down what went wrong. Don't repeat the same mistake
- Develop your own style. What works for someone else may not work for you

## Lindy and Time

**Lindy effect:**
- For non-perishable things, life expectancy increases with age. The longer something has survived, the longer it's expected to survive
- Protocols that have survived years of attacks, bear markets, and exploits are more likely to persist
- New, untested projects have the highest mortality rate regardless of how promising they appear
- Apply Lindy thinking to: protocols, consensus mechanisms, tokenomics models, and DeFi primitives
- Time is the ultimate stress test. Nothing replaces having survived

**Time as stress test:**
- Small, repeated stresses make systems stronger. Prolonged calm makes fragile systems more dangerous
- Markets look most bullish when they're about to break, and most bearish at the bottom

**Via negativa:**
- It is easier and more robust to identify what's wrong than what's right. Knowledge grows by subtraction
- Eliminating bad trades matters more than finding great ones
- A checklist of reasons NOT to trade is more valuable than a checklist of reasons to trade
- When evaluating a thesis, focus first on what could make it fail
- The winning strategy is often: do less, avoid more
